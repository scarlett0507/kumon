About Topography

Topography is the study of the land surface. The topography of an area could refer to the surface forms and features themselves or a description in maps. For example, topography refers to mountains, valleys, rivers, or craters on the surface.

The elevation is the distinguishing factor for topographic maps. In GIS, we use digital elevation models (DEM) for the terrain. Nine out of ten topographic maps show contour lines, just lines of equal elevation.

Application of topography

Topography in Maps

Each mapping agency designs its topographic maps with a specific goal in mind. For example, constructing a new highway might drive a topographic map to feature woodland cover, soil types, or rock classification along the route. Over time, topographic map series often get periodic updates.

Topography Uses

Topographic maps show how rivers flow, how high mountains rise, and how steep valleys descend. They lay out the land such as in these examples:

Engineers use topographic maps to plan a road, construct a cell tower, or plan a hydroelectric dam.

Geologists use topography to understand the tectonic activity, landforms, and where to dig a mine.

Hikers use topographic maps to find trails and slope steepness to plan their ascent.

Astronomers study topography outside Earth-like on the moon, Mars, or an asteroid.

Climate scientists tie topography into climate models to recognize air and water flow.

Topography of Japan

Mountains and Volcanoes

The topography of the Japanese Archipelago is intensely undulating with mountainous areas, occupying some three-fourths of the country. The highest point in the country is Mount Fuji, a volcano dormant since 1707 that rises to 3,776 m above sea level in Shizuoka Prefecture.

About 200 volcanoes are forming the volcanic belts of Chishima, Chokai, Nasu, Fuji, Norikura, Daisen, and Kirishima. Earthquakes frequently occur in the archipelago. Destructive earthquakes occur several times a century.

Plains

There are three significant plains in central Honshū. The largest is the Kantō Plain in the Kantō region. The capital Tokyo and the largest metropolitan population is located there. The second-largest plain is the Nōbi Plain. The third-largest plain is the Osaka Plain in the Kinki region. It features the second-largest urban area of Osaka.

Hokkaido has multiple plains: Ishikari Plain, Tokachi Plain, Kushiro Plain, and Sarobetsu Plain. Another important plain is the Sendai Plain around the city of Sendai in northeastern Honshū.

Rivers

Rivers are generally steep and swift, and few are suitable for navigation except in their lower reaches. Although most rivers are less than 300 km in length, their rapid flow from the mountains provides hydroelectric power. Seasonal variations in flow have led to extensive development of flood control measures.

Coast

The coast is the area where land meets the ocean. Coasts are crucial zones in natural ecosystems, often home to a wide range of biodiversity. Japan is the fourth-largest island country in the world and the largest island country in East Asia. It has the 6th longest coastline at 29,751 km.

Glacial

A glacier forms where the accumulation of snow exceeds its ablation over many years. Glaciers slowly deform and flow under stresses induced by their weight, creating crevasses, seracs, and other distinguishing features. Scientists have found three glaciers in Toyama Prefecture, the first recognized in Japan and the southernmost in East Asia.
