About River

A river is a natural flowing watercourse, usually freshwater, flowing towards an ocean, sea, lake, or another river.

Water generally collects in a river from precipitation through a drainage basin from surface runoff and other sources such as groundwater recharge, springs, and the release of stored water in natural ice.

The river only covers around 0.1% of the land on Earth. They are made more evident and significant to humans since many human cities and civilizations are built around the freshwater supplied by rivers and streams.

Types of Rivers

Subsurface Rivers

Not all rivers flow on the surface. Subterranean rivers flow underground in caves or caverns. Such rivers are frequently found in regions with limestone geologic formations.

Intermittent River

An intermittent river only flows occasionally and can be dry for several years at a time. These rivers are found in regions with limited or highly variable rainfall or can occur because of geologic conditions such as a highly permeable river bed. Some intermittent rivers flow during the summer months but not in the winter.

Uses of Rivers

Construction Material

The coarse sediments, gravel, and sand generated and moved by rivers are extensively used in construction.

Energy Production

Fast-flowing rivers and waterfalls are widely used as energy sources via watermills and hydroelectric plants.

Food Source

Rivers have been a source of food since pre-history. Navigation and Transport

Rivers have been used for navigation for thousands of years. The earliest navigation evidence is found in the Indus Valley Civilization, which existed in northwestern India around 3300 BC.

Political Borders

Rivers have been important in determining political boundaries and defending countries. For example, the Mississippi in North America and the Rhine in Europe are major east-west boundaries in those continents.

Rivers in Japan

The rivers of Japan are characterized by their relatively short lengths and considerably steep slopes due to the narrow and mountainous terrain. The Mogami, the Fuji, and the Kuma are regarded as Japan's three most rapid rivers.

The longest river is the Shinano, which flows from Nagano to Niigata. The Tone has the most significant watershed and serves water to more than 30 million inhabitants of the Tokyo area.
