About Typhoon

A typhoon is an extensive storm system with a circular or spiral system of violent winds, typically hundreds of kilometers or miles in diameter. For stronger typhoons, a characteristic structure called the "eye" forms when the maximum wind speeds exceed about 85 miles per hour or 140 kilometers per hour. The eye is a region of clear air with no clouds and is a few tens of kilometers in diameter.

The energy that powers typhoons come from the evaporation of warm ocean water. Warmer ocean water produces more powerful typhoons, growing into "super typhoons."

The Location of the Typhoon

A typhoon is developed between 180° and 100°E in the Northern Hemisphere.

Requirements for Typhoon Formation

(1) sufficiently warm sea surface temperatures;

(2) atmospheric instability;

(3) high humidity in the lower to middle levels of the troposphere;

(4) enough Coriolis effect of developing a low-pressure center;

(5) a pre-existing low-level focus or disturbance;

(6) a low vertical wind shear.

Typhoon Season

While most storms form between June and November, a few storms may occur between December and May. On average, the northwestern Pacific features the most numerous and intense tropical cyclones globally.

Typhoon Tips

Before the Typhoon

　

・Know the evacuation routes for frequented locations.

・Make sure phones, portable chargers, and other devices are fully charged.

・Bring in outdoor objects that could be blown away by heavy winds.

・Make sure to close windows and avoid sleeping near glass ones.

・Prepare a supply of water and nonperishable food. Check expiration dates annually.

・Write down significant phone numbers and store them in the emergency kit.


During the Storm

・If unable to evacuate and in a place that is vulnerable to flooding, make sure to stay on the second floor.

・Check tropical storm advisories from the Japan Meteorological Agency.

・Stay away from downed power lines. When exposed to water, electric facilities could cause a power outage and electric shock hazard.
